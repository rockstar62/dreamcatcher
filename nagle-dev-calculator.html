Stone Mill — Development Pricing Framework

Nagle-correct volume discount structure for custom and tract developments

Nagle's core warning on volume discounts

Nagle's research across hundreds of industrial pricing cases found one consistent pattern: volume discounts are the single most margin-destructive tool in B2B sales. Not because the math is wrong — but because of three structural traps that almost always follow.

Trap 1 — You discount the promise, not the performance
A builder commits to 40 homes at a discounted price. You hold the discount. They build 18 homes and the development stalls. You gave away margin on every house they did build, and you never got the volume that justified it.
Trap 2 — The discount becomes the new floor
Once a builder buys at $14, $17 feels like a price increase on the next project. Nagle calls this "price memory." The discount you gave for volume becomes the reference price for everything that follows — even when volume doesn't.
Trap 3 — Volume discounts signal price elasticity
When you offer a volume discount unprompted, you are telling the buyer that your price has room. They didn't know that before. Now they'll test it on every transaction, not just volume situations.
What Nagle recommends instead

Nagle doesn't say never discount for volume. He says the discount must only pay out when the volume is real, delivered, and verifiable — not promised. The mechanism is a retroactive rebate tied to verified purchase milestones, not a price reduction on invoice.

The structural difference: earn-back vs. give-away. An upfront price reduction is a give-away. A retroactive rebate is an earn-back. Earn-back protects your margin floor and keeps the builder motivated to hit the number — because the money comes after delivery, not before.

The large development risk profile — where Nagle is most urgent

Large developments are built in phases over 2–5 years. Phase 1 might be 10 homes. Phase 2 gets delayed. A builder who committed to 40 homes might only pull stone for 12 in year one. If you priced on 40-home volume from day one, you gave away margin for volume that arrives slowly, irregularly, and possibly never in full.

The phase problem
A 40-home development delivers stone orders 8–12 homes at a time, 12–18 months apart. Each phase is effectively a standalone purchase. If you priced on 40-home volume from day one, you gave away $3–$5/sqft on phase 1 orders that have zero guarantee phase 2 ever happens.
The material switch risk
At homes 15–20, a large developer may value-engineer stone out entirely, switch to cheaper manufactured stone, or renegotiate your price down further. You absorbed the risk; they kept the option.

The Nagle-correct deal structure — both scenarios
What you charge
Full direct price on every invoice. No discount on invoice. The builder sees full rate. This protects price memory — they never see a lower price as their reference point.
What you give back
A rebate check after verified sqft milestones are hit. Quarterly or per-phase. The check is the reward for delivered volume — not a promise on future volume.
The milestone trigger
Small dev: 2,500 sqft in a rolling 12 months. Large dev: each phase delivery verified by invoice total. No milestone = no rebate. No negotiation.
The floor — non-negotiable
$3/sqft markup over wholesale is your hard floor. Below this you're doing dealer margin with direct-channel overhead. It does not make business sense at any volume.

Script 1 — Opening a development conversation (don't lead with price)
"Before we talk numbers, let me ask you something. What's your biggest headache on the stone side when you're running a development? Because in my experience, most builders aren't sweating the price per foot nearly as much as they're sweating whether the stone shows up on schedule and whether it matches across houses. If I can solve those two things, price is usually the easy part of this conversation."
Script 2 — When a builder asks for a volume price upfront
"Here's how I work on developments. I don't quote a development price before the volume is real, because I've been burned by builders who committed to 40 homes and built 12. What I do is this: you buy at standard rate on phase one. I track every foot you take. When you hit the milestone, I cut you a check back. That check is your volume discount — earned, not promised. Works better for both of us."
Script 3 — Presenting good/better/best for a development
"What I'd suggest for a development your size is thinking about it in three tiers. Your standard lots get limestone — strong material, great price point. Your premium and corner lots step up to fieldstone — that's what differentiates your tier-one lots. Your model home gets granite cobble — that's what sells the whole development because every buyer who walks the model wants it. You set the mix, I supply all three. Your average stone cost blends somewhere in the middle, and you've got three price points in the market."
Script 4 — Protecting your floor when a builder pushes hard
"My floor is three dollars a foot over my wholesale cost. Below that I'm doing dealer margin with direct overhead and it doesn't make sense for me. Above that, there's room to talk depending on how the volume actually lands. But I'm not going to give you a number today based on 40 homes that might take five years to build. Give me phase one. I'll take care of you on phase two based on what actually happened."
Script 5 — Selling supply guarantee as the real value
"Here's what most builders don't think about until it's a problem. You're 18 homes into a 40-home development and the stone you spec'd doesn't match anymore — the quarry changed the lot, the color shifted. Now homes 19 through 40 don't match homes 1 through 18. That's a real problem in a prestige development. What I can do for a committed development relationship is reserve your material from the same deposit from day one. That's worth more than a dollar a foot discount. That's the thing that protects your reputation."
