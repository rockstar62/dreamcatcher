The Direct B2B Model — Why It Wins

Wholesale distributors exist to solve a logistics and relationship problem — they aggregate product and manage contractor accounts so manufacturers don't have to. You already have a direct relationship with masons and contractors. You have a quarry. You have no reason to give away $4–5/sq ft.

At $35M revenue and $13/sq ft average, you're moving roughly 2.7 million square feet per year. At $8.50 wholesale that same volume is $22.9M. The direct model is worth an additional $12M+ in revenue at scale — and because it's margin, not cost, it drops almost entirely to EBITDA. This is the strategic moat.

The constraint: direct B2B at scale requires a sales infrastructure that wholesale distribution replaces. That's exactly what the Roff-Marsh machine is designed to build.

TOC Applied to Stone Mill

Theory of Constraints
The Five Focusing Steps — Applied Now

Goldratt's Five Focusing Steps are not a philosophy. They are a diagnostic. Run them on Stone Mill today and the answer is unambiguous.

STEP 1
Identify the Constraint

Market awareness is the constraint. You have quarries with capacity. You have a product priced correctly. You have a team. The system is not producing more revenue because not enough of the right contractors, masons, and builders in the right geographies know Stone Mill exists. This is a pure market constraint — and TOC says a market constraint means you are not exploiting your current capacity.

STEP 2
Exploit the Constraint

Get maximum output from what you already have before adding anything. You have a contact list. You have HubSpot live. You have a cold call playbook. You have a Mafia Offer to build (see next tab). The immediate goal: activate the existing system at full throttle — 20+ prospecting touches per week — before hiring, before expansion, before anything else. You are currently operating the sales machine at roughly 10% capacity.

STEP 3
Subordinate Everything Else

Every other function supports the constraint. Production schedules, quarry output planning, order fulfillment — all of it should be paced to what sales can generate, not the other way around. Right now your ops likely have spare capacity. Don't optimize ops. Don't build a better quarry process. Sell. Everything subordinates to filling the pipeline.

STEP 4
Elevate the Constraint

When you've maxed out what one person can do, add capacity to the constraint. This is the Sales Process Coordinator hire (Q3 Year 1), geographic expansion (Year 2), and eventually a dedicated sales rep for new markets (Year 3). You only elevate after exploiting — hiring before the system is running full throttle wastes money and creates confusion.

STEP 5
Repeat

Once market awareness is no longer the constraint, find the new one. At $7M it will likely be production capacity or geographic reach. At $14M it may be management depth. At $24M it may be capital for acquisition. Each stage of this roadmap begins by identifying the new constraint before assuming what the next investment should be.

Layers of Resistance — Your Sales Psychology Map

Every contractor or builder you call moves through predictable resistance layers before saying yes. TOC named them. Here's how they apply to selling real stone direct:

Layer 1 "I don't agree this is a problem"
→
The contractor is happy with his current stone supplier or doesn't think about stone sourcing as a problem. Your job: surface the pain. "What happens on your timeline when your stone supplier runs out of the material you spec'd mid-project?"
Layer 2 "I don't agree that's the direction to go"
→
They agree there's an issue but aren't sure direct from quarry is the answer. Your job: the Mafia Offer. Show them the math — $4–5/sq ft savings, guaranteed consistency, one call to the quarry owner.
Layer 3 "I don't believe it will work"
→
They like the idea but doubt you can deliver. Your job: proof. Case studies, photos, referrals from contractors they respect. The fourth-generation story is credibility, not just brand.
Layer 4 "I see negative side effects"
→
They worry about switching friction — what if you run out of stock, what about lead times, what about quality consistency? Your job: answer before they ask. Build those answers into the offer structure.
Layer 5 "I don't know how to implement"
→
They're interested but the change feels like work. Your job: remove the friction completely. Sample delivery, spec sheet, one-page ordering process. Make saying yes easier than staying with the distributor.

The Mafia Offer

TOC — Market Constraint
What a Mafia Offer Actually Is

Goldratt's definition: an offer so good, so clearly in the customer's interest, that they would feel foolish refusing it. It is not a discount. It is not a promotion. It is a restructured offer that removes the customer's biggest fear and replaces it with a compelling reason to act now.

The question to answer: What does a mason, contractor, or custom builder fear most in the stone procurement process? That is where your Mafia Offer begins.

What Contractors Actually Fear

Based on the direct B2B selling context, the real fears are:

  • → Material inconsistency mid-project — ordering stone and getting a different lot that doesn't match what's already on the wall
  • → Supply gaps that stop a job — the distributor runs out, the timeline blows up, the homeowner is angry
  • → Paying for fake stone — getting upsold cultured stone by a distributor who makes more margin on it, then having a homeowner complain years later
  • → Margin squeeze — stone is a significant line item; every dollar saved is a dollar of profit or competitive pricing room
"Real Michigan stone, from our quarry to your job site — guaranteed lot-matched, guaranteed available, at $4–5 less per square foot than your distributor. If the stone doesn't match your spec exactly, we replace it. No questions."

This offer works because it attacks all four fears simultaneously: consistency (lot-matched guarantee), supply (guaranteed available), authenticity (from our quarry — not concrete with paint on it), and margin (the $4–5/sq ft savings is real money on a 2,000 sq ft project).

The math for a contractor: A 2,000 sq ft project at $8.50 wholesale = $17,000 material cost. At $13 direct from Stone Mill = $26,000. That's not savings for them — that's a price increase. This means the Mafia Offer works differently by segment.

For masons and contractors who currently buy at wholesale and mark up: you're competing against their distributor relationship, not the price. The offer has to be about reliability, consistency, and the story they get to tell their client — real Michigan stone from a fourth-generation quarry. For custom builders who spec the stone: they pass cost to the homeowner, so your $13–15 is fine — they want the brand story and the guarantee. That's where your volume lives.

Segment Clarity — This Is Critical

Masons/contractors buying for their own margin: Hard sell at retail pricing. They're buying at $8.50 and marking up. You can't ask them to pay $13 unless the story is strong enough to justify it to their client. Target these with a contractor pricing tier at $10–11 with volume commitment.

Custom builders speccing for a client: They pass cost through. Your $13–15 is fine. They want the design story, the Michigan provenance, the real stone guarantee. This is your primary B2B target — high margin, right buyer, aligns with premium positioning.

Architects and designers: They specify stone without paying for it. Getting specified by one architect can mean 10 years of repeat projects. The offer here is education and relationship — get on their approved vendor list and the orders follow automatically.

Claude can: Build every sales tool, script, playbook, and sequence. Draft all outbound emails, follow-ups, and proposals. Research target markets, competitors, and acquisition targets. What a Real Board Would Tell You

No Filter
Truth #1 — The Math Is Aggressive

$1.7M to $35M in five years is a 20x revenue increase. That is not impossible — but it requires either consistent 83% year-over-year growth, or organic growth plus at least one acquisition. Organic alone likely tops out at $15–20M in five years if everything goes right. Getting to $35M almost certainly requires buying something — a competitor, a quarry, a distributor — in Years 3 or 4. Plan for it now. That is not failure; that is how platforms are built.

Truth #2 — The 70% Customer Is the Only Emergency

Everything in this strategy assumes the large customer stays. If they leave or cut orders in half tomorrow, the business goes from $1.7M to $510K revenue. That is not a recoverable position without the reserve you have. Do not let a single week go by without actively adding new customers. This is not optional. It is the primary job until that number is below 25%.

Truth #3 — Your Quarry Capacity Is the Unknown Variable

You said the quarries are "about right for where we are now." That means they support $1.7M. Getting to $7M means roughly 4x the volume. That either requires significant equipment investment, expanding the quarries, signing sourcing agreements with the two quarries you don't own, or all three. You need a hard production ceiling number before you can commit to any revenue target. Get that from your operations team this quarter.

Truth #4 — Pricing Is Your Biggest Lever and Your Biggest Risk

You're at $13–15/sq ft in a market where wholesale is $8.50. That spread is where your margin lives. The risk: if a large distributor decides to compete on quality and story — or a regional competitor emerges — that spread compresses. The defense is brand depth, contractor relationships, and the Michigan quarry story being impossible to replicate. Start building that brand infrastructure now while no one is chasing you.

Truth #5 — You Are the Bottleneck Right Now

Not your quarries. Not your team. Not your capital. You. Every hour you spend doing things that aren't prospecting, qualified conversations, or strategic decisions is an hour the business doesn't grow. The Coordinator hire in Q3 Year 1 is not a convenience — it is the single most important operational decision of Year 1. Until someone else owns outbound and admin, you will remain the constraint no matter how good the system looks on paper.

Truth #6 — The Exit Number Depends on Things You Control

At $7M EBITDA and 8x, that's a $56M exit. At 6x (because margins slipped or customer concentration crept back up or the management team isn't there), it's $42M. At 10x (because you ran a competitive process, had clean books, and a proven team), it's $70M. The multiple is not luck — it is the product of decisions you make between now and Year 4. Customer concentration, management depth, clean financials, documented processes, and no owner dependency are all worth 1–3 turns on the multiple. Build them deliberately.
