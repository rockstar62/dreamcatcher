The Reality — No Filter

Board Priority #1
⚠ What a Buyer Sees When They Open Your Books

One entity. 70% of $1.7M. No contract. Handshake only. Reseller model.

A PE firm or strategic buyer doing due diligence sees this and one of two things happens: they walk, or they reprice. A 2–3 turn multiple discount is standard for concentration above 25%. At $7M EBITDA and 8x that's a $56M exit. At 5x due to concentration risk that's $35M. This one problem costs you $21M at exit if it's not fixed.

The relationship being strong doesn't matter on paper. The customer's goodwill is not a contractual obligation. Any buyer's counsel will flag it immediately.

The Two-Track Problem

This tree has to solve two things simultaneously — and they pull in opposite directions if handled wrong:

Track 1 — Protect the anchor. The reseller relationship is strong and represents real revenue. The goal is NOT to lose them while you diversify. Handled wrong, a push for a contract or a price change signals distrust and damages the relationship.

Track 2 — Diversify fast enough to matter. Getting from 70% to 25% while the anchor stays flat means you need to grow total revenue from $1.7M to roughly $4.8M with zero growth from the anchor — or grow the anchor slowly while adding new revenue much faster.

"The goal is not to fire the big customer. The goal is to grow around them until they're no longer the whole story."

The Anchor Strategy

Protect First
What the Anchor Customer Actually Is

A reseller who buys from Stone Mill at wholesale and marks up to their own customers. Long relationship. No contract. Strong personally. This is a common structure in building materials — and it's also the structure most vulnerable to a single personnel change, a business sale, or a supplier switch on their end.

The risk isn't that they're unhappy with you. The risk is that something changes in THEIR business that has nothing to do with you. They get acquired. Their key buyer leaves. They pivot to a different stone type. None of those events require them to be dissatisfied with Stone Mill.

The Preferred Supplier Agreement — How to Approach It

This is the single most valuable thing you can do for the exit without disrupting the relationship. A one-page document that says three things:

  • → Stone Mill commits to priority supply allocation for the anchor — their orders are filled before new accounts in a capacity-constrained situation.
  • → Pricing stability — a defined rate or a defined notice period before any price change. Gives them certainty. Costs Stone Mill almost nothing.
  • → Annual review commitment — both parties agree to meet once a year to review the relationship, volume, and pricing. Formalizes the partnership without creating legal rigidity.
How to raise it: "We're doing some planning for the next few years and want to make sure we have the right structure to serve you well as you grow. Would you be open to putting a simple preferred supplier arrangement on paper — nothing complicated, just something that protects both of us?"

Most long-term B2B partners welcome this. It signals professionalism and commitment. If they resist, that itself is important information about the relationship's actual durability.

What to Learn From the Anchor Conversation

Before any agreement discussion, have the relationship conversation first. You need to know:

  • → Are they growing, stable, or at risk in their own market?
  • → Is there a succession plan in their business — or is it also owner-dependent?
  • → Are they happy with current pricing, volume, and product range?
  • → Do they have any concerns about Stone Mill's ability to serve them as they grow?

Their answers tell you whether this relationship deserves a preferred supplier agreement or whether the risk is higher than the surface suggests.

Where This Strategy Breaks

Assumption Audit
🔴
The anchor exits or significantly reduces orders during Years 1–2. If the 70% customer cuts volume by half while you're mid-diversification, total revenue could drop to $1.35M and the growth narrative collapses. This is the scenario with no good answer except having diversified faster. Every week you don't prospect is a week closer to this being unrecoverable.
URGENT
🔴
The anchor resists any formalization. If they decline even a simple preferred supplier letter, you have a relationship that a buyer cannot rely on. That doesn't mean the relationship is bad — but it means the exit story has a structural hole that no amount of revenue growth repairs. You need to know their answer before Year 1 Q2.
URGENT
⚠️
New accounts don't reach $150K average — they stay small. If new accounts average $50–75K instead of $150K, you need 40+ accounts to hit the concentration target, not 20. That's a different sales machine — more accounts, more coordination, more complexity. The coordinator hire becomes essential even faster.
WATCH
⚠️
Prospecting volume doesn't hit 20 touches/week consistently. The math on getting to 20 accounts in 24 months requires sustained outreach. If the playbook runs at 50% capacity — 10 touches/week — the timeline doubles and Year 2 concentration targets slip to Year 3. This is a Steve execution risk, not a market risk.
WATCH
⚠️
New reseller accounts create a new concentration cluster. If three resellers each at 15% replace the one reseller at 70%, the risk isn't fully solved — a segment shock (economic downturn hits stone retail) could hit all three simultaneously. True diversification requires mixing resellers with direct contractors and architects.
WATCH
✅
The anchor stays stable during diversification. Steve rates the relationship as very strong with no signs of change. Given the reseller depends on Stone Mill's product for their own business, they have as much incentive to maintain the relationship as Stone Mill does. This assumption is reasonable — not guaranteed, but reasonable.
